The first human space launch from US soil since 2011. The first ever private human spaceflight. The beginning of a new era for space travel.

These headlines don't capture the depth and significance of this milestone. It may be hard to understand how returning to the International Space Station — which has already happened consistently for the past twenty years — is anything close to the magnificence of Neil Armstrong taking steps on the Moon more than fifty years ago. How is this simple mission, SpaceX Demo-2, truly the beginning of a new era?

Let's take a look at some history.

In 1957, the Soviet Union shocked the world with the launch of *Sputnik* into orbit. The United States feared the domination of their Cold War rival and sought to rapidly expand their space program with the founding of NASA. As time went on, the US was still consistently behind the Soviet Union in achieving additional space milestones. Unsatisfied, President John F. Kennedy called for the most ambitious goal yet: landing on the Moon in less than a decade.

NASA peaked with the Apollo lunar program. The grand total of $25.4 billion makes Apollo one of the most costly nonmilitary US government projects to date. The massive feat of landing humans on the Moon in 1969, matching JFK's timeline, pushed the best engineers to the limit to maximize the potential of 1960's technology. The completion of Apollo, and the failure of the USSR to match it, marked the end of the Space Race. As public support declined and NASA's budget was slashed, the US would go on to develop the Space Shuttle and coordinate with Russia on the International Space Station.

The Space Race was, quite simply, a competition. Each country strived to be better than the other.

Competition has its perks. Competition encourages innovation. Competition between companies spawns better products and keeps prices down for consumers. Competition fostered the rapid development seen in the 1960's Space Race. In just eight years, the US went from launching Alan Shepard to orbit to landing Neil Armstrong on the Moon. And it is the absence of competition in the past few decades that has crippled advancements in space exploration.

But competition is finally back.

Although it has not met the public eye, the spaceflight industry has been changing. One important development in 2008 was the $1.6 billion Commercial Resupply Services contract granted to SpaceX from NASA to ship cargo to the ISS in preparation for the retirement of the Space Shuttle. This partnership, in addition to the contract with Orbital ATK (now under Northrop Grumman), has allowed recurring cargo resupply to the ISS through the last decade.

The Commercial Resupply program was followed by the Commercial Crew program to launch astronauts to the Space Station. After a series of competitions, the two final contracts were $2.6 billion for SpaceX and $4.2 billion for Boeing to develop their crew capsules. Each company had to meet a series of milestones before starting human launch services, and the crewed demo mission (Demo-2 for SpaceX) is the last milestone. Boeing, meanwhile, failed to reach the ISS on their uncrewed demo mission, pushing back their first crewed launch to 2021.

{% include image.html url="/spacex-demo-2/commercial-crew-astronauts-combined.jpg" alt-text="Commercial Crew astronauts pose in front of Dragon and Starliner" caption="The first set of Commercial Crew astronauts pose in front of the SpaceX Crew Dragon and Boeing Starliner, both of which plan to regularly transport astronauts to the ISS." source="NASA" source-url="https://www.flickr.com/photos/nasahqphoto/42038482360/" %}

Commercial Resupply and Commercial Crew mark the beginning of NASA's changing relationship with private companies.

The old system of commercialization of space has contractors develop individual parts, but NASA ultimately coordinates the project as a whole. The problem is that NASA is a federal government agency, and ambitious space missions require lots of time and political support. With each changing administration and Congress, the agenda often changes before the work is finished. This explains why NASA constantly changes their priorities, from Moon colonization to Mars colonization and back. This also explains why NASA must continue to use the same contractors from across the States. Politics is in control.

The new system of commercialization of space changes the government's role. NASA sets certain criteria, and private contractors submit proposals to receive a contract to develop their solution. NASA supplies the money and basic requirements, but the companies are the ones in control. If NASA decides to abandon the project, the companies still receive the money to develop the system for their own purposes.

SpaceX takes advantage of this. Since 2002, they have had the same leadership of Elon Musk and the same goal to colonize Mars, but their side projects with NASA allow them to develop their technologies and achieve smaller milestones while still keeping the money flowing in. NASA's budget also benefits because of the small overhead and extra funding from SpaceX.

We are now seeing the results of the technological development and innovation from this new system through Demo-2. Gone are the countless buttons and switches from the cramped Apollo Lunar Module and Space Shuttle. The touchscreen displays and spacious interior parallel the technological and hardware design developments that have been made in other industries since the 60's. In addition, the new spacesuits designed by SpaceX are made-to-fit, lightweight, and of course, sleek. The camera views of the two astronauts riding inside the futuristic spacecraft are straight out of Hollywood.

{% include image.html url="/spacex-demo-2/crew-dragon-interior.jpg" alt-text="Crew Dragon Interior" caption="Astronauts Bob Behnken and Doug Hurley sit onboard the Crew Dragon." source="SpaceX" source-url="https://www.flickr.com/photos/spacex/49927519643/" %}

The pictures don't tell the whole story. Every aspect of the Falcon 9 and Dragon system is modern and reliable, from autonomous control to the built-in abort system to the most important feature, reusability. Five years ago, many claimed that it would be impossible to propulsively land a rocket on a drone ship in the middle of the Atlantic. Now, that is expected of SpaceX.

Vertical integration also allows SpaceX greater efficiency and speed from concept to production. This, combined with advanced technologies and reduced costs from reusability, allowed SpaceX to beat Boeing to the Space Station despite receiving *less* money from NASA for the Commercial Crew program. SpaceX plans to use the knowledge gained from Falcon 9 and Crew Dragon towards [Starship](https://www.spacex.com/vehicles/starship/), an ambitious, fully reusable human transport system designed to colonize Mars and beyond.

Although they are currently in the spotlight, it is important to note that SpaceX is not the only new contender in spaceflight. [Blue Origin](https://www.blueorigin.com), headed by Jeff Bezos, is working to provide affordable space tourism with their current New Shepard and upcoming New Glenn rockets. [Rocket Lab](https://www.rocketlabusa.com), based out of New Zealand, has already completed several missions on their Electron rocket, designed to launch small satellites at an affordable rate to make space more accessible for small organizations. Both are developing similar reusable modern systems through vertical integration to stay competitive.

Compare this with companies like Boeing, Lockheed Martin, and Northrop Grumman, the big names of aerospace who are sitting in the past. They are riding the backs of Congressmen for continued government support despite slow development speed and low innovation. They still have great engineers and abundant resources, but they are building off old technologies like the Space Shuttle. While they do focus elsewhere in aerospace, the kings of the Shuttle era are quickly falling behind in the spaceflight industry.

NASA senses this shift. The new faces are getting their fair share in the increasing number of NASA contracts for the Artemis lunar program. They granted SpaceX a contract to launch to the Gateway, the upcoming lunar space station. They also granted Rocket Lab a contract to launch a small satellite to lunar orbit in preparation for Gateway. The most bizarre result was the recent human lunar lander contract: Boeing failed to receive money despite SpaceX and Blue Origin winning out (Dynetics, another contractor, also received money). In particular, the selection of SpaceX's Starship over Boeing is bizarre because it is complete overkill for NASA's criteria and it directly competes with NASA's Space Launch System and Orion capsule, for which Boeing, Lockheed Martin, and Northrop Grumman are primary contractors.

It is clear that NASA sees these new public-private partnerships as beneficial, albeit with indirect enthusiasm through their contract-based competitions to ensure continued funding from Congress. Putting companies up against each other in a somewhat friendly competition helps achieve their goals more quickly. The free market brings down costs, benefiting government agencies who need spy satellites, companies who need communication satellites, and citizens who just want to experience zero-g.

SpaceX cannot dominate this industry alone, or else competition diminishes. Competition is necessary for the healthy growth of this new space economy. It is critical that Blue Origin, Rocket Lab, and other spaceflight companies stay in the running. The old aerospace giants especially must pivot soon to face this new challenge. If they do not, they will be so incredibly outpaced that it will be impossible to catch up.

This new space race is not limited to just the private sector. Ever since the Shuttle was retired in 2011, Russia has been the only country capable of launching humans to the International Space Station. NASA has paid Russia about $90 million per astronaut just to send them there.

Now, the United States has brought human spaceflight back home, at an even cheaper ticket price of $60 million through SpaceX. As Elon Musk jokingly remarked, ["the trampoline is working."](https://www.space.com/elon-musk-spacex-launch-trampoline-comment-explained.html) Other countries like Canada and Japan will once again send their astronauts on US rockets. Now without a monopoly on ISS launches, Russia will need to enhance their space program or concede to the US.

This was not just another rocket launch. The Demo-2 mission validates the ongoing effort of SpaceX and NASA and finally brings them into the limelight. SpaceX has paved the way for private competition, government competition, and more public-private partnerships in space with their state-of-the-art technologies. This is truly the beginning of a second space race, this time without the fear of a nuclear apocalypse. A sustainable return to the Moon, the first habitat on Mars, and space tourism for the general population are all now closer than ever. The world should be getting excited.
